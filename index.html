Introduction

The solar system is a collection of celestial objects bound by gravity to a central star, the Sun.

The solar system consists of the Sun, eight planets, moons, asteroids, comets, and other celestial objects bound by gravity, all orbiting around the Sun.

The discovery of the solar system as we understand it today evolved over centuries, with key contributions from early astronomers.

The first major breakthrough came from Nicolaus Copernicus in the 16th century, who proposed the heliocentric model, suggesting that the planets, including Earth, revolve around the Sun, challenging the earlier geocentric model (Earth-centered) by Ptolemy. Later, Johannes Kepler and Galileo Galilei provided further evidence supporting this model through observations and mathematical laws, solidifying the concept of the solar system.

  • The Sun: A medium-sized star at the center of the solar system, providing the necessary light and heat to sustain life on Earth.
  • Planets: Eight major planets orbit the Sun in elliptical paths.
  • Dwarf Planets: Smaller celestial bodies, such as Pluto, that orbit the Sun but do not clear their orbit of other debris.
  • Moons: Many planets have moons (natural satellites) orbiting them. For example, Earth has one moon, while Jupiter has over 80.
  • Asteroids: Mostly found in the asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter, asteroids are small rocky bodies that also orbit the Sun.
  • Comets: Icy bodies that originate from the outer regions of the solar system. When they approach the Sun, they develop glowing tails due to the heat.
  • The Kuiper Belt and Oort Cloud: Regions beyond Neptune filled with icy bodies and comets.
Sun

The Sun is the central star of the solar system and by far the largest object within it.

The Sun's immense size, gravity, and energy output sustain the entire solar system, driving processes like Earth's climate and the orbits of planets.

  • Type: Yellow dwarf (G-type main-sequence star)
  • Mass: 1.989 × 10³⁰ kg (about 99.86% of the total mass of the solar system)
  • Age: Approximately 4.6 billion years
  • Composition: Mostly hydrogen (about 74%) and helium (about 24%), with small amounts of heavier elements.
Mercury

Closest to the Sun, Mercury has an extremely thin atmosphere and experiences drastic temperature changes between its day and night sides. Its surface is heavily cratered, resembling the Moon.

  • Type: Terrestrial planet
  • Size: Smallest planet
    • Surface area: 74.8 million km²
Venus

Known as Earth’s “sister planet,” Venus has a thick atmosphere composed mainly of carbon dioxide, creating a runaway greenhouse effect, making it the hottest planet. It has dense clouds of sulfuric acid and rotates in the opposite direction to most planets.

  • Type: Terrestrial planet
  • Size: Similar to Earth in size
    • Surface area: 460.2 million km²
Earth

The only planet known to support life, Earth has a diverse climate, a protective atmosphere rich in nitrogen and oxygen, and liquid water covering about 70% of its surface. It has one natural satellite, the Moon.

  • Type: Terrestrial planet
  • Size: Largest of the terrestrial planets
    • Surface area: 510.1 million km²
Mars

Known as the “Red Planet” due to iron oxide (rust) on its surface, Mars has polar ice caps, large volcanoes (like Olympus Mons), and vast canyon systems. Scientists are studying its potential to support past or future life.

  • Type: Terrestrial planet
  • Size: Smaller than Earth
    • Surface area: 144.8 million km²
Jupiter

Jupiter is known for its massive size, strong magnetic field, and iconic Great Red Spot, a giant storm larger than Earth. It has over 80 moons, including Ganymede, the largest moon in the solar system.

  • Type: Gas giant
  • Size: Largest planet
    • Surface area: 61.42 billion km²
Saturn

Famous for its extensive ring system, Saturn is mostly made of hydrogen and helium. It has numerous moons, with Titan being the largest, and it possesses a thick atmosphere with strong winds and storms.

  • Type: Gas giant
  • Size: Second-largest planet
    • Surface area: 42.7 billion km²
Uranus

Uranus has a unique feature: it rotates on its side, likely due to a massive collision. Its blue-green color comes from methane in its atmosphere. Uranus has faint rings and numerous moons.

  • Type: Ice giant
  • Size: Third-largest planet
    • Surface area: 8.1 billion km²
Neptune

Neptune is the farthest planet from the Sun and has strong winds, including the fastest in the solar system. Its deep blue color also comes from methane, and it has rings and moons, with Triton being the largest.

  • Type: Ice giant
  • Size: Slightly smaller than Uranus
    • Surface area: 7.6 billion km²
